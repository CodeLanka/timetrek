Anuradhapura

Anuradhapura, named after 'Anuradha' senevi who built one of the first cities after King Vijaya landed at Thambapanni has been a pinnacle of development and culture in Sri Lankan histroy.

Sigiri Alakamandawa

Sigiriya is a UNESCO world heritage site which has been the awe and wonder of many archelogists and engineers alike. The water supply from the top of the rock to the lower rock gardens work even today during the rainy season, displaying an ornate or water fountains at the foot of the site.
Although some argue that Sigiriya is a fortress which King Kashyapa came to hide from his brother, it is not a logical explanation. This is bears markings of many generations of usage, and using a dead-end for protection is not explainable.

Art

The Sigiri frescoes have been visited by thousands of tourists from many countries. The technology that made such an artwork last for such a long time is by all means state-of-the-art.

Mechanical wonders

Carved support edifices still exist on the side of the rock, which show that a wooden elevator has been used centuries ago by the King to climb up to the area on top of the rock. Some historical sources state this elevator was named "Karkataka Yanthra", loosely translating to crablike machine.

Smooth Carvings

Almost all stone carvings at the Sigiriya archeological site are smooth surfaces. One could even wonder whether the rock was as smooth as butter when it was cut to carve the features. The stone throne room is a good example where a large sized rock is cut off from the main rock. How this a large sized rock was cut and how it was moved aside nowhere to be seen, is still a mistery.
